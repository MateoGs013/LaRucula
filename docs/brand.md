# LaRucula — Brand Foundation

## Brand thesis
LaRucula is not a generic seaside restaurant brand.

It should feel like a contemporary Mediterranean dining house with editorial taste:
- warm but not rustic
- luxurious but not cold
- artistic but not chaotic
- immersive but still usable

The site should suggest that the restaurant has point of view, spatial character, and a very clear sense of hospitality.

## Freedom clause
This document defines the feeling to hit, not a locked ingredient list.

Claude may change:
- the palette
- the typography pairing
- the image system
- the graphic language
- the layout rhythm

if the final result is more modern, more memorable, and still clearly feels like LaRucula.

## What current award-level hospitality work signals
Based on the Awwwards and hospitality research in `docs/awwwards-analysis.md`, the strongest current references tend to share these traits:
- story-first presentation instead of brochure-style information dumping
- very strong scale contrast between oversized display type and quiet utility text
- immersive photography that sells atmosphere, not just dishes
- local identity translated through materiality and narrative, not cliché icons
- luxury communicated through composition, restraint, and pacing

LaRucula should adopt those signals without becoming trend-chasing.

## Emotional territory
The brand should feel:
- sun-warmed
- cultivated
- tactile
- atmospheric
- composed
- refined
- sensorial
- memorable

## The kind of modernity we want
Prefer:
- quiet modernity
- tactile minimalism
- cinematic editorial rhythm
- heritage refreshed with contemporary restraint
- premium atmosphere with human warmth

Avoid:
- glossy tech-luxury coldness
- boho beach clichés
- rustic nostalgia
- generic beige “boutique hotel” sameness
- maximalism for the sake of novelty

## Visual vocabulary
- oversized serif statements
- asymmetric composition with clear focal points
- image windows that feel framed and intentional
- layered photography with supporting detail crops
- premium negative space
- subtle hospitality metadata
- restrained seal, monogram, or numbering cues
- tactile surfaces, warm shadows, and material contrast
- architecture and interiors carrying equal weight with food

## Color direction
Primary palette:
- parchment sand
- warm ivory
- toasted brown
- mineral stone
- muted green or sage as contrast
- dusk charcoal for depth

Color behavior:
- warm neutrals should dominate the base canvas
- one cooler or greener counterweight should prevent the site from feeling flat
- deep dark sections should be used sparingly to reset attention
- saturated accents should stay limited and intentional

These color cues are starting points, not mandatory brand colors.
A sharper, darker, cleaner, or more unexpected palette is allowed if it still feels premium, Mediterranean, and authored.

## Typography direction
- display serif should feel elegant, cultured, and slightly fashion-adjacent
- sans-serif should be calm, precise, and secondary
- handwritten accents should be rare and intimate
- hierarchy should come from scale, spacing, and measure before decoration
- major headings should often do most of the emotional work

## Content tone
Write like a confident host with taste.

Prefer:
- evocative phrasing
- precise restraint
- short blocks with real atmosphere
- place-based storytelling

Avoid:
- ad copy clichés
- “best experience ever” language
- generic luxury wording
- long explanatory paragraphs with no tension

## Narrative arc
Most pages should loosely move through this rhythm:
1. arrival
2. appetite
3. atmosphere
4. ritual
5. invitation

## Brand keywords
Mediterranean / editorial hospitality / dining house / tactile / atmospheric / refined / contemporary warmth / sea light / authored luxury / modern ritual
